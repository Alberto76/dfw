A Supposedly Fun Thing I’ll Never Do Again

A Supposedly Fun Thing I’ll Never Do Again is Wallace’s 1997 collection of nonfiction, his first book to compile his now-famous essays. It includes personal and persuasive essays, as well as long-form journalism.

The book’s title essay, originally published in Harper’s as “Shipping Out”, concerns Wallace’s one-week cruise in the Caribbean aboard the MV Zenith, which Wallace renames the Nadir. The essay describes the excesses of the cruise and its crew’s hospitality, and the irony of a week of relaxation that leads to deep internal despair. The essay makes extensive use of footnotes for asides, clarifications, and even narrative, in what has become a key feature of Wallace’s unique style.

The book’s other essays include “Derivative Sport in Tornado Alley”, Wallace’s coming-of-age-through-tennis story, and “E Unibus Pluram”, an essay concerning the influence of television and America’s culture of irony on contemporary literature.

This collection of “essays and arguments” — originally published in Harper’s, Esquire and Premiere, among other magazines — reveals Mr. Wallace in ways that his fiction has of yet managed to dodge: as a writer struggling mightily to understand and capture his times, as a critic who cares deeply about “serious” art, and as a mensch.
